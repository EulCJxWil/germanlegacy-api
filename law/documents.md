# Required Documents for German Citizenship Applications

Derived from analysis of ~500 sent emails across all case types.

---

## Universal Rules (all pathways)

- Documents written in English do **not** need to be translated into German
- No apostille is required
- Each document is needed **only once**, even if multiple applicants are submitting together
- Internet records, photos, and regular (uncertified) copies are not accepted — originals or certified copies are required
- German civil registry records: submit the certified copy received from the German registry office
- US birth/marriage certificates: bring original to German consulate, they make a certified copy, you keep the original; or request a new certified copy from the issuing office (vitalchek.com)
- Passports and driver's licenses: a regular uncertified copy is sufficient (except for the applicant submitting in person, who brings the original for the consulate to copy)
- FBI background check: print and submit the PDF received by email, or submit the physical letter

---

## FBI Background Check Rule

This is the most important pathway-specific rule:

| Pathway | FBI background check required? |
|---|---|
| StAG §5 | **Yes** — required for every applicant who is not yet a German citizen |
| Art. 116 GG | **No** |
| StAG §15 | **No** |
| Feststellung | **No** (applicant is already German) |

Reason: §5 is naturalization by declaration — German law requires a background check for all naturalizations. Art. 116 GG and §15 are restorations of citizenship that was wrongly stripped — the applicant is treated as having been German all along, so no naturalization background check applies. Feststellung applicants are already German citizens.

Exception: If one member of a group applying together is already a confirmed German citizen (e.g. grandmother who never lost citizenship), that person does **not** need an FBI background check, even if the rest of the group does.

## Per-Generation Documents (the lineage chain)

For **every person** in the lineage chain between the German ancestor and the applicant:

- **Birth record** with the names of both parents — to establish parentage and connect links in the chain
- **Marriage record** — to establish the next generation was born in wedlock (relevant for citizenship inheritance rules), and to document name changes

Exception: if a couple was never married (out of wedlock), no marriage record is needed for that couple — but the birth record of their child must show the mother's name.

## Pathway-Specific Documents

### Feststellung (Outcome 1)
*The applicant is already a German citizen by unbroken descent through the father's line.*

Additional documents needed for the emigrant ancestor:
- **German birth record** of the ancestor
- **Passenger arrival record** (ship manifest) — to document when the ancestor emigrated; request certified copy from NARA: https://www.archives.gov/research/immigration/passenger-arrival.html
- **Proof that the ancestor did NOT become a US citizen before the key descendant was born**, one of:
  - US Certificate of Naturalization (showing naturalization date was AFTER the descendant's birth)
  - Naturalization file from USCIS: https://www.uscis.gov/records/genealogy (shows naturalization date)
  - Certificate of Non-Existence from USCIS (if the ancestor never naturalized): https://midas.uscis.dhs.gov/#/cne/request

---

### StAG §5 (Outcome 3)
*Citizenship was denied due to sex discrimination — typically German mother + foreign father in wedlock 1949–1974, or German grandmother who lost citizenship on marriage to a foreigner.*

Core documents for the German ancestor (grandmother or great-grandmother):
- **German birth record**
- **Proof of German citizenship**: German passport, German ID card (Personalausweis/Kennkarte), population register record (Melderegister/Einwohnermeldekartei), or auxiliary file to the marriage register (Sammelakte zum Heiratsregister). If no passport/ID is available, the Sammelakte is the most reliable way to get proof — it is the file kept by the German civil registry office that contains all documents submitted when the couple applied for a marriage license, which often includes a birth record with citizenship notation or a Staatsangehörigkeitsausweis.
- **Marriage record** (to prove the next generation was born in wedlock to the German grandmother + foreign grandfather)
- **US Certificate of Naturalization or naturalization file** — to document WHEN the grandmother naturalized as a US citizen. Critical for cases where the applicant was born close in time to the naturalization: the applicant must have been born **before** the grandmother's naturalization date to qualify.

Special case — if grandmother married after April 1, 1953:
- No loss of citizenship on marriage (she remained German)
- Naturalization date is still needed to verify applicant was born before naturalization
- No need to prove she didn't lose citizenship on marriage

Special case — if grandmother married between May 24, 1949 and March 31, 1953 and held only German citizenship:
- She did NOT lose citizenship on marriage (statelessness protection under Art. 16 GG)
- However, confirmation is needed that she held no other citizenship at the time of marriage (e.g. no Consular Report of Birth Abroad showing she was also a US citizen at birth)
- A Consular Record of Birth Abroad (CRBA) for the German-born parent who was born in the US may be needed to show they acquired US citizenship at birth (not through naturalization), which distinguishes them from applicants who would need a §5 analysis

### Art. 116 GG (Outcome 2)
*Ancestor was explicitly denaturalized by the Nazis — either by the Eleventh Decree (all Jews living outside Germany Nov 27, 1941 – May 8, 1945) or by individual listing in the Reichsanzeiger.*

Core documents for the denaturalized ancestor:
- **German birth record**
- **Proof that the ancestor was Jewish or subject to Nazi persecution** — one or more of:
  - German vital records (birth, marriage certificate) listing religion as "israelitischer Religion," "mosaischer Religion," or similar
  - Entry in the Gedenkbuch (Memorial Book of victims of Nazi persecution): https://www.bundesarchiv.de/gedenkbuch/
  - Entry in the Yad Vashem database
  - Forced name addition ("Israel" or "Sara") on German documents
  - Document from a persecuted organization membership
- **Proof of when the ancestor left Germany** (to establish they were living outside Germany during the Eleventh Decree period): ship/arrival record, or other document establishing residence outside Germany after 1941
- **US naturalization document** (Certificate of Naturalization or naturalization file) — to understand the sequence, though for Art. 116 GG the ancestor was already stateless when they naturalized (the Eleventh Decree denaturalized them in 1941)
- **German marriage record** if applicable

Note: For Art. 116 GG, the documents needed to prove persecution can be flexible. Multiple combinations of documents may work; it is not necessary to have all of them. Submitting all available documents is recommended.

---

### StAG §15 (Outcome 4)
*Ancestor fled Nazi persecution and lost citizenship for persecution-related reasons (but may not qualify under Eleventh Decree — e.g. they fled and naturalized before November 1941, or they were persecuted on political/religious grounds that are not covered by the Eleventh Decree).*

Same as Art. 116 GG plus:
- **Additional documentation of the persecution-related loss**: reason for flight (e.g. political persecution, religious persecution), documentation that the naturalization abroad was caused by the persecution (e.g. the ancestor fled, then naturalized — the sequence matters)

In practice: if both Art. 116 GG and §15 apply, use Art. 116 GG — the BVA prefers it and it is free with no citizenship surrender requirement.

## Per-Applicant Documents

### Adult applicant (18 or older)

- **Birth certificate** with the names of both parents (to connect the applicant to the lineage chain)
- **Marriage certificate** (if married) — to document name change and to prove connection to applicant's children
- **Divorce record** (if divorced and the marriage is relevant to the lineage chain or name)
- **Passport or driver's license** (ID proof)
- **FBI background check** (§5 only; see table above)
- **Name change documentation** if the name on the ID differs from the name on the birth certificate (e.g. marriage certificate, court order for name change)

### Minor child (under 18)

- **Birth certificate** with the names of both parents
- **Passport or driver's license** (if old enough to have one; not required for very young children)
- **Passport or driver's license of the other parent** (if the child is younger than 16 and both parents share custody)
- **Marriage certificate of the parents** (if parents married — to establish wedlock status)
- No FBI background check required for minors

## Special Document Situations

### Proving German citizenship of the German ancestor when no passport survives

Most common approach: request the **Sammelakte zum Heiratsregister** (auxiliary file to the marriage register) from the German civil registry office that recorded the grandparents' marriage. This file contains all documents the couple submitted when applying for a marriage license, including proof of citizenship of the German party.

Other options:
- **Melderegister/Einwohnermeldekartei** (population register) — can show citizenship status and emigration date
- **Lohnsteuerkarte** (tax card)
- **Kennkarte** (German ID card issued 1938–1945)
- **Any official German document** that states the person is a German citizen

### Ship records (passenger arrival manifests)

Used in both Feststellung and persecution cases to document when the ancestor emigrated. Request a certified copy from NARA: https://www.archives.gov/research/immigration/passenger-arrival.html

### US naturalization records

- Certificate of Naturalization: the physical document issued by the court
- Naturalization file: the full file kept by USCIS including petition, declaration of intention, and court records — request at https://www.uscis.gov/records/genealogy
- Either is acceptable to prove the naturalization date
- If the ancestor never naturalized: Certificate of Non-Existence from USCIS: https://midas.uscis.dhs.gov/#/cne/request

### Consular Record of Birth Abroad (CRBA)

Required in some §5 cases where a German-American child was born in the US to one German parent. The CRBA proves the child acquired US citizenship at birth (by descent from the American parent), not through naturalization. This is relevant when the question is whether the child lost German citizenship as a minor when a parent naturalized: if the child acquired US citizenship at birth (from the other parent), then separately the parent's naturalization would have caused the child to also lose German citizenship. If they only had German citizenship, the minor exception applies.
